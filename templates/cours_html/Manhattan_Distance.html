The Manhattan Distance: A Fundamental Metric in Discrete Geometry

Introduction and Motivations

The Manhattan distance, also known as the \(L_1\) norm or taxicab metric, is a fundamental concept in discrete geometry and has profound implications in various fields such as computer science, operations research, and physics. Originating from the grid-like layout of streets in Manhattan, this metric measures the distance between two points in a grid-based path, where movement is restricted to horizontal and vertical directions only. This notion emerges naturally in problems involving pathfinding, clustering, and optimization on discrete grids.

The Manhattan distance is indispensable in scenarios where Euclidean distance (the straight-line distance) is not applicable or practical. For instance, in city planning and transportation systems, the Manhattan distance provides a more accurate model of travel distances compared to Euclidean distance. Additionally, in digital image processing and computer vision, the Manhattan distance is used for template matching and feature detection due to its robustness against rotational variations.

Definitions

To understand the Manhattan distance, consider two points \(A\) = \((x_1, y_1)\) and \(B\) = \((x_2, y_2)\) in a two-dimensional grid. Intuitively, the Manhattan distance between \(A\) and \(B\) is the sum of the absolute differences of their Cartesian coordinates. This can be generalized to higher dimensions.

Let \(A\) and \(B\) be two points in an \(n\)-dimensional space, with coordinates \(A = (a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n)\) and \(B = (b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_n)\). The Manhattan distance \(d\) between \(A\) and \(B\) is defined as: \[d(A, B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} |a_i - b_i|\]

This definition can be expressed using quantifiers as follows: \[\forall A, B \in \mathbb{R}^n, d(A, B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} |a_i - b_i|\]

Theorems

Several important theorems and properties are associated with the Manhattan distance. One notable theorem is the triangle inequality, which holds for the Manhattan distance as it does for other metrics.

For any three points \(A\), \(B\), and \(C\) in an \(n\)-dimensional space, the following inequality holds: \[d(A, C) \leq d(A, B) + d(B, C)\]

To prove this theorem, consider the definition of the Manhattan distance and the properties of absolute values.

Proofs

Proof. Let \(A = (a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n)\), \(B = (b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_n)\), and \(C = (c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_n)\) be three points in \(\mathbb{R}^n\). The Manhattan distance between \(A\) and \(C\) is: \[d(A, C) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} |a_i - c_i|\]

By the triangle inequality for absolute values, we have: \[|a_i - c_i| \leq |a_i - b_i| + |b_i - c_i|\]

Summing over all \(i\) from 1 to \(n\), we get: \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} |a_i - c_i| \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} (|a_i - b_i| + |b_i - c_i|)\]

This can be split into two sums: \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} |a_i - c_i| \leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} |a_i - b_i| + \sum_{i=1}^{n} |b_i - c_i|\]

Which is equivalent to: \[d(A, C) \leq d(A, B) + d(B, C)\]

Thus, the triangle inequality holds for the Manhattan distance. ◻

Properties and Corollaries

The Manhattan distance exhibits several important properties that make it useful in various applications.

  1. Symmetry: The Manhattan distance is symmetric, meaning \(d(A, B) = d(B, A)\) for any two points \(A\) and \(B\).

  2. Non-negativity: The Manhattan distance is non-negative, i.e., \(d(A, B) \geq 0\) for any two points \(A\) and \(B\), with equality if and only if \(A = B\).

  3. Triangle Inequality: As proven earlier, the Manhattan distance satisfies the triangle inequality.

These properties ensure that the Manhattan distance is a valid metric, making it suitable for use in metric spaces and various mathematical analyses.

Conclusion

The Manhattan distance is a versatile and fundamental metric with wide-ranging applications in discrete geometry, computer science, and other fields. Its simplicity and robustness make it an essential tool for modeling and solving problems involving grid-based pathways and discrete spaces. Understanding the properties and theorems associated with the Manhattan distance provides a solid foundation for further exploration in these areas.
