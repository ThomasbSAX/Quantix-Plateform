Theil’s U Statistic: A Measure of Inequality

Introduction and Motivations

The study of economic inequality has been a central theme in economics for centuries. One of the most influential contributions to this field is Theil’s U statistic, introduced by Henri Theil in 1967. This measure of inequality is based on the concept of entropy and provides a way to quantify the degree of disparity within a distribution.

The need for such a measure arises from the limitations of traditional inequality indices like the Gini coefficient. Theil’s U statistic offers several advantages, including its decomposability and subadditivity properties, which make it particularly useful for comparing inequality across different groups or over time.

In this article, we will explore the historical context, mathematical foundations, and applications of Theil’s U statistic. We will also discuss its properties and how it compares to other measures of inequality.

Definitions

To understand Theil’s U statistic, we first need to define some key concepts.

Entropy

Consider a probability distribution \(P = (p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n)\), where \(p_i \geq 0\) and \(\sum_{i=1}^n p_i = 1\). The entropy of this distribution is defined as:

\[H(P) = -\sum_{i=1}^n p_i \log p_i\]

Entropy measures the uncertainty or randomness of the distribution. A higher entropy indicates a more equal distribution, while a lower entropy suggests greater inequality.

Theil’s U Statistic

Now, let’s consider a population with \(n\) individuals and a variable of interest \(X\), such as income. Let \(x_i\) denote the value of \(X\) for the \(i\)-th individual, and let \(\mu\) be the mean of \(X\). The share of the \(i\)-th individual in the total income is given by:

\[s_i = \frac{x_i}{\sum_{j=1}^n x_j}\]

Theil’s U statistic is then defined as:

\[T = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{x_i}{\mu} \log \left( \frac{x_i}{\mu} \right)\]

Alternatively, using the shares \(s_i\), we can write:

\[T = \sum_{i=1}^n s_i \log \left( \frac{1}{s_i} \right)\]

This statistic can be interpreted as a measure of the divergence between the observed distribution and a perfectly equal distribution, where each individual has an equal share.

Theorems

Decomposability of Theil’s U Statistic

One of the most important properties of Theil’s U statistic is its decomposability. This property allows us to break down the overall inequality into contributions from different subgroups.

Let \(T\) be Theil’s U statistic for the entire population, and let \(T_g\) be the within-group inequality for group \(g\). Let \(S_g\) be the share of group \(g\) in the total population, and let \(T_b\) be the between-group inequality. Then:

\[T = \sum_g S_g T_g + T_b\]

where \(T_b = \sum_g S_g \log \left( \frac{\mu}{\mu_g} \right)\), and \(\mu_g\) is the mean income of group \(g\).

Proof. We start by expressing the overall entropy in terms of the within-group and between-group components:

\[H = \sum_g S_g H_g + H_b\]

where \(H_g\) is the entropy within group \(g\), and \(H_b\) is the between-group entropy. Using the definition of Theil’s U statistic, we can write:

\[T = 1 + \frac{1}{n} H\]

Substituting the expression for \(H\), we get:

\[T = 1 + \frac{1}{n} \left( \sum_g S_g H_g + H_b \right)\]

Now, we can express \(T_g\) and \(T_b\) in terms of \(H_g\) and \(H_b\):

\[T_g = 1 + \frac{1}{n_g} H_g\] \[T_b = \sum_g S_g \log \left( \frac{\mu}{\mu_g} \right)\]

Substituting these expressions back into the equation for \(T\), we obtain:

\[T = \sum_g S_g T_g + T_b\] ◻

Subadditivity of Theil’s U Statistic

Another important property of Theil’s U statistic is its subadditivity. This property ensures that the overall inequality is less than or equal to the sum of the inequalities within each subgroup.

Let \(T\) be Theil’s U statistic for the entire population, and let \(T_g\) be the within-group inequality for group \(g\). Then:

\[T \leq \sum_g S_g T_g\]

Proof. We start with the decomposability theorem:

\[T = \sum_g S_g T_g + T_b\]

Since \(T_b \geq 0\) by definition, we have:

\[T \leq \sum_g S_g T_g\]

This completes the proof. ◻

Properties and Corollaries

Properties of Theil’s U Statistic

  1. Boundedness: Theil’s U statistic is bounded between 0 and \(\log n\), where \(n\) is the number of individuals in the population. A value of 0 indicates perfect equality, while a value of \(\log n\) indicates maximum inequality.

  2. Sensitivity to Transfers: Theil’s U statistic is sensitive to transfers between individuals with different incomes. Specifically, it satisfies the Pigou-Dalton principle of transfers, which states that a transfer from a richer to a poorer individual should decrease the measure of inequality.

  3. Additivity: Theil’s U statistic is additive across groups, meaning that the overall inequality can be expressed as the sum of within-group and between-group inequalities.

Corollaries

Theil’s U statistic is invariant to changes in the population size, as long as the relative shares remain constant.

Proof. Let \(n\) be the original population size, and let \(m\) be the new population size. The shares \(s_i\) remain unchanged, so:

\[T = \sum_{i=1}^n s_i \log \left( \frac{1}{s_i} \right) = \sum_{i=1}^m s_i \log \left( \frac{1}{s_i} \right)\] ◻

Theil’s U statistic is invariant to changes in the unit of measurement for the variable of interest.

Proof. Let \(c\) be a positive constant. Then:

\[T = \sum_{i=1}^n s_i \log \left( \frac{1}{s_i} \right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{c x_i}{c \sum_{j=1}^n x_j} \log \left( \frac{c \sum_{j=1}^n x_j}{c x_i} \right) = \sum_{i=1}^n s_i \log \left( \frac{1}{s_i} \right)\] ◻

Conclusion

Theil’s U statistic is a powerful and versatile measure of inequality that has found widespread application in economics and other social sciences. Its decomposability and subadditivity properties make it particularly useful for comparing inequality across different groups or over time. Moreover, its sensitivity to transfers and invariance properties ensure that it provides a robust and meaningful measure of inequality.

In future research, it would be interesting to explore the relationship between Theil’s U statistic and other measures of inequality, as well as its potential applications in policy analysis and decision-making.
