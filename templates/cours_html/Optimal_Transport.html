Optimal Transport: A Comprehensive Study

Introduction and Motivations

Optimal Transport (OT) is a mathematical framework that seeks to find the most efficient way to transport resources from one location to another. This problem, initially formulated by Gaspard Monge in 1781, has evolved into a powerful tool with applications ranging from economics and statistics to machine learning and image processing.

The OT problem arises naturally in various contexts. For instance, consider a scenario where you need to distribute goods from multiple warehouses to retail stores. The goal is to minimize the total transportation cost while meeting the demand at each store. This is precisely what OT aims to solve.

Definitions

Before delving into the formal definitions, let’s build some intuition. Suppose we have two probability measures, \(\mu\) and \(\nu\), representing the distribution of resources and demands, respectively. We want to find a way to match points from \(\mu\) to points from \(\nu\) such that the total cost of transportation is minimized.

Now, let’s formalize this intuition. Let \((X, d)\) be a metric space and \(\mu\), \(\nu\) be two probability measures on \(X\). We denote by \(\Pi(\mu, \nu)\) the set of all couplings of \(\mu\) and \(\nu\), i.e., the set of all measures \(\gamma\) on \(X \times X\) such that for any measurable set \(A \subseteq X\), we have \[\gamma(A \times X) = \mu(A) \quad \text{and} \quad \gamma(X \times A) = \nu(A).\]

Definition 1. The Optimal Transport problem is to find a coupling \(\gamma \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)\) that minimizes the total cost: \[\inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int_{X \times X} d(x, y) \, d\gamma(x, y).\]

This can be written more explicitly as: \[\inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int_{X} \int_{X} d(x, y) \, d\gamma(x, y).\]

Theorems

One of the fundamental results in Optimal Transport is the existence and uniqueness of optimal transport plans under certain conditions. Let’s build up to this result.

First, consider the case where \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are both absolutely continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure. In this case, we can use the concept of a transport map.

Theorem 2 (Brenier’s Theorem). Let \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) be two probability measures on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) such that \(\mu\) is absolutely continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure. Then, there exists a unique convex function \(T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\) such that the gradient \(\nabla T\) pushes forward \(\mu\) to \(\nu\), i.e., \[(\nabla T)_\# \mu = \nu.\] Moreover, the optimal transport plan is given by \((Id, \nabla T)_\# \mu\).

Proofs

Let’s sketch the proof of Brenier’s Theorem. The key idea is to use the concept of convex duality.

Proof. We start by considering the Kantorovich dual problem: \[\sup_{\phi, \psi} \int \phi \, d\mu + \int \psi \, d\nu\] subject to \(\phi(x) + \psi(y) \leq d(x, y)\) for all \(x, y \in \mathbb{R}^n\).

By convex duality, the optimal value of this problem is equal to the infimum in the primal OT problem. The key insight is that when \(\mu\) is absolutely continuous, the optimal \(\phi\) and \(\psi\) can be chosen to be convex functions.

Let \(T\) be the convex conjugate of \(\phi\), i.e., \(T(y) = \sup_{x} \{ \langle x, y \rangle - \phi(x) \}\). Then, the condition \(\phi(x) + \psi(y) \leq d(x, y)\) implies that \(T\) is the gradient of \(\phi\).

The uniqueness of \(T\) follows from the strict convexity of the cost function \(d(x, y) = |x - y|^2\). ◻

Properties and Corollaires

Let’s list some important properties of optimal transport plans.

  1. If \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are both absolutely continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure, then the optimal transport plan is induced by a transport map \(T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n\) such that \((\nabla T)_\# \mu = \nu\).

  2. The optimal transport cost is always achieved, i.e., there exists a \(\gamma \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)\) that attains the infimum.

  3. If \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are both supported on a compact set, then the optimal transport plan is unique.

Corollary 3. Let \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) be two probability measures on \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Then, the optimal transport cost is finite if and only if there exists a constant \(C\) such that for all \(x \in \text{supp}(\mu)\) and \(y \in \text{supp}(\nu)\), we have \(|x - y| \leq C\).

Proof. The forward direction is straightforward. For the reverse direction, consider a sequence of points \(x_n \in \text{supp}(\mu)\) and \(y_n \in \text{supp}(\nu)\) such that \(|x_n - y_n| \to \infty\). Then, the optimal transport cost is at least \(\sum_{n} |x_n - y_n|\), which diverges. ◻

Conclusion

Optimal Transport is a rich and active area of research with numerous applications. In this article, we have provided an introduction to the basic concepts and results in OT. We hope that this will serve as a starting point for further exploration of this fascinating subject.
