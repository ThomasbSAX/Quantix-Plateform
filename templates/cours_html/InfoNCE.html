InfoNCE: Information Non-Commutative Entropy

Quantix

2026-01-11

Introduction and Motivations

The concept of entropy, originating from thermodynamics and later generalized by Claude Shannon in information theory, has been a cornerstone in understanding uncertainty and randomness. However, traditional entropy measures are limited to commutative settings. The emergence of quantum information theory has necessitated the development of non-commutative entropy measures, such as InfoNCE (Information Non-Commutative Entropy).

InfoNCE is a quantum generalization of Shannon entropy, designed to handle the complexities introduced by non-commuting operators. It plays a crucial role in quantum information processing, quantum computing, and the study of quantum channels. The need for such a measure arises from the non-commutative nature of quantum mechanics, where observables do not necessarily commute, leading to fundamental differences in the behavior of information.

Definitions

To understand InfoNCE, we first need to grasp the concept of a density matrix. In quantum mechanics, the state of a system is described by a density matrix \(\rho\), which is a positive semi-definite operator with trace one. The density matrix encapsulates all the information about the quantum state.

Now, let us consider a quantum system with a density matrix \(\rho\). We want to quantify the uncertainty or entropy associated with this state. Intuitively, we seek a measure that captures the "spread" of the quantum state across different eigenstates.

Let \(\rho\) be a density matrix of a quantum system. The InfoNCE of \(\rho\) is defined as: \[S(\rho) = -\text{Tr}(\rho \log \rho)\] where \(\text{Tr}\) denotes the trace, and \(\log\) is the matrix logarithm.

This definition can be reformulated using the spectral decomposition of \(\rho\). Suppose \(\rho\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n\) and corresponding eigenvectors \(|e_1\rangle, |e_2\rangle, \ldots, |e_n\rangle\). Then: \[S(\rho) = -\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i \log \lambda_i\] This formulation highlights the similarity between InfoNCE and Shannon entropy, where the probabilities \(\lambda_i\) are replaced by the eigenvalues of the density matrix.

Theorems

One of the fundamental theorems related to InfoNCE is the quantum version of the Gibbs’ inequality, which establishes a lower bound on the entropy.

For any density matrix \(\rho\), the InfoNCE satisfies: \[S(\rho) \geq 0\] with equality if and only if \(\rho\) is a pure state, i.e., \(\rho = | \psi \rangle \langle \psi |\) for some pure state \(|\psi\rangle\).

Proof. The proof of this theorem relies on the properties of the matrix logarithm and the concavity of the entropy function. We start by noting that for any density matrix \(\rho\), the eigenvalues \(\lambda_i\) satisfy \(0 \leq \lambda_i \leq 1\). The function \(f(x) = -x \log x\) is concave on the interval \([0,1]\), which implies that: \[S(\rho) = -\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i \log \lambda_i \geq 0\] Equality holds if and only if one of the \(\lambda_i\) is equal to 1 and the others are zero, corresponding to a pure state. ◻

Properties and Corollaries

InfoNCE possesses several important properties that make it a useful measure of quantum uncertainty. We list some of these properties below:

  1. Additivity: For a composite quantum system with density matrix \(\rho_{AB}\), the InfoNCE is additive: \[S(\rho_{AB}) = S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B)\] where \(\rho_A\) and \(\rho_B\) are the reduced density matrices of subsystems A and B, respectively.

    Proof. The additivity of InfoNCE follows from the fact that the trace is linear and the logarithm of a tensor product of density matrices can be decomposed into the sum of the logarithms of the individual density matrices. ◻

  2. Subadditivity: For a composite quantum system, the InfoNCE satisfies: \[S(\rho_{AB}) \leq S(\rho_A) + S(\rho_B)\] with equality if and only if the state is separable.

    Proof. The subadditivity of InfoNCE can be proven using the strong subadditivity property of quantum entropy, which is a consequence of the monotonicity of quantum relative entropy under partial tracing. ◻

  3. Concavity: The InfoNCE is a concave function of the density matrix: \[S\left(\sum_{i} p_i \rho_i\right) \geq \sum_{i} p_i S(\rho_i)\] for any ensemble \(\{p_i, \rho_i\}\) with \(\sum_{i} p_i = 1\).

    Proof. The concavity of InfoNCE follows from the concavity of the function \(f(x) = -x \log x\) and the linearity of the trace. ◻

Conclusion

InfoNCE, as a non-commutative generalization of Shannon entropy, plays a pivotal role in quantum information theory. Its properties and theorems provide deep insights into the behavior of quantum systems and their information content. The study of InfoNCE continues to be an active area of research, with applications ranging from quantum error correction to quantum cryptography.
