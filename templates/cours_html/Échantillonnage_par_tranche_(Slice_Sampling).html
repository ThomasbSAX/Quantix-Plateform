Slice Sampling: A Comprehensive Study

Introduction et Motivations

The concept of slice sampling emerges from the need to efficiently sample from complex, high-dimensional probability distributions. Traditional methods such as Metropolis-Hastings can be inefficient or difficult to implement when dealing with distributions that lack a simple analytical form. Slice sampling, introduced by Radford M. Neal in 2003, provides an elegant and efficient alternative.

Slice sampling is particularly useful in Bayesian statistics, where the goal is to sample from the posterior distribution of model parameters. The method is versatile and can be applied to a wide range of distributions, making it a valuable tool in the statistical physicist’s arsenal.

Définitions

To understand slice sampling, let us first consider the problem of sampling from a probability density function (pdf) \(\pi(x)\). The idea behind slice sampling is to transform this problem into one of sampling uniformly from the region under the curve defined by \(\pi(x)\).

Suppose we have a target pdf \(\pi(x)\). We can define a new variable \(y\) such that the joint distribution of \((x, y)\) is uniform over the region \(\{ (x, y) : 0 \leq y \leq \pi(x) \}\). This region is often referred to as the "slice" or "sub-graph" of \(\pi(x)\).

Formally, the joint distribution can be written as: \[p(x, y) = \frac{\mathbb{I}(0 \leq y \leq \pi(x))}{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \pi(x) \, dx}\] where \(\mathbb{I}\) is the indicator function.

The marginal distribution of \(y\) is uniform on \([0, \max(\pi(x))]\), and the conditional distribution of \(x\) given \(y\) is: \[p(x | y) = \frac{\mathbb{I}(0 \leq y \leq \pi(x))}{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \mathbb{I}(0 \leq y \leq \pi(x)) \, dx}\]

Théorèmes

One of the key theorems in slice sampling is the following:

Let \(\pi(x)\) be a probability density function. Then, the joint distribution \(p(x, y)\) defined by: \[p(x, y) = \frac{\mathbb{I}(0 \leq y \leq \pi(x))}{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \pi(x) \, dx}\] has the property that the marginal distribution of \(x\) is \(\pi(x)\).

Proof. To prove this theorem, we need to show that the marginal distribution of \(x\) under \(p(x, y)\) is indeed \(\pi(x)\).

First, note that the joint distribution \(p(x, y)\) is uniform over the region \(\{ (x, y) : 0 \leq y \leq \pi(x) \}\). The marginal distribution of \(x\) is obtained by integrating out \(y\): \[p(x) = \int_{0}^{\pi(x)} p(x, y) \, dy = \int_{0}^{\pi(x)} \frac{\mathbb{I}(0 \leq y \leq \pi(x))}{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \pi(x) \, dx} \, dy = \frac{\pi(x)}{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \pi(x) \, dx}\] Since \(\pi(x)\) is a probability density function, we have: \[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \pi(x) \, dx = 1\] Thus, \(p(x) = \pi(x)\), which completes the proof. ◻

Preuves

The proof of the slice sampling theorem relies on the properties of the indicator function and the definition of the joint distribution. By integrating out the variable \(y\), we recover the original target distribution \(\pi(x)\). This shows that slice sampling preserves the desired marginal distribution.

Propriétés et Corollaires

Slice sampling has several important properties and corollaires that make it a powerful tool for statistical sampling.

  1. Uniform Sampling: The joint distribution \(p(x, y)\) is uniform over the slice defined by \(0 \leq y \leq \pi(x)\). This property ensures that the sampling process is unbiased.

  2. Versatility: Slice sampling can be applied to a wide range of distributions, including those that are multimodal or have complex shapes. This makes it particularly useful in Bayesian statistics.

  3. Efficiency: Slice sampling can be more efficient than traditional methods such as Metropolis-Hastings, especially when dealing with high-dimensional distributions. This is because it avoids the need for tuning proposal distributions.

Each of these properties contributes to the robustness and efficiency of slice sampling, making it a valuable tool in statistical physics and Bayesian inference.

Conclusion

Slice sampling is an elegant and efficient method for sampling from complex probability distributions. Its ability to transform the problem of sampling from \(\pi(x)\) into one of sampling uniformly from a slice makes it particularly versatile and powerful. The properties and theorems associated with slice sampling ensure that it remains a valuable tool in the statistical physicist’s arsenal.
