Completeness Score: A Measure of Data Integrity

Quantix

2026-01-11

Introduction and Motivations

In the realm of data science and machine learning, the quality of data is paramount. One crucial aspect of data quality is completeness, which refers to the extent to which all required data is present and accounted for. The Completeness Score emerges as a quantitative measure to assess this very aspect.

The need for such a metric arises from the fact that incomplete data can lead to biased models, unreliable analyses, and ultimately, poor decision-making. By quantifying completeness, data scientists can identify gaps in their datasets and take appropriate measures to address them.

Definitions

To understand what we aim to achieve with the Completeness Score, consider a dataset where some entries are missing. Our goal is to have a measure that reflects how much of the required data is present.

Formally, let \(\mathcal{D}\) be a dataset with \(n\) records and \(m\) attributes. We denote by \(\mathcal{D}_i\) the \(i\)-th record in \(\mathcal{D}\), and by \(\mathcal{D}_{ij}\) the value of the \(j\)-th attribute for the \(i\)-th record.

The Completeness Score of a dataset \(\mathcal{D}\) is defined as the proportion of non-missing values across all attributes and records. Mathematically, it can be expressed as: \[\text{Completeness Score}(\mathcal{D}) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{m} \mathbb{I}(\mathcal{D}_{ij} \neq \text{missing})}{n \times m}\] where \(\mathbb{I}\) is the indicator function, which returns 1 if the condition inside is true and 0 otherwise.

Alternatively, the Completeness Score can be seen as the average completeness across all attributes: \[\text{Completeness Score}(\mathcal{D}) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{j=1}^{m} \left( \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{I}(\mathcal{D}_{ij} \neq \text{missing})}{n} \right)\]

Theorems

While the Completeness Score is a straightforward measure, it is essential to understand its properties and implications.

Let \(\mathcal{D}_1\) and \(\mathcal{D}_2\) be two datasets with the same schema but potentially different completeness. If \(\mathcal{D}_1\) has at least as many non-missing values as \(\mathcal{D}_2\), then: \[\text{Completeness Score}(\mathcal{D}_1) \geq \text{Completeness Score}(\mathcal{D}_2)\]

Proof. Assume that \(\mathcal{D}_1\) has at least as many non-missing values as \(\mathcal{D}_2\). That is, for all \(i\) and \(j\): \[\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{m} \mathbb{I}(\mathcal{D}_{1,ij} \neq \text{missing}) \geq \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{m} \mathbb{I}(\mathcal{D}_{2,ij} \neq \text{missing})\] Dividing both sides by \(n \times m\) yields: \[\text{Completeness Score}(\mathcal{D}_1) \geq \text{Completeness Score}(\mathcal{D}_2)\] ◻

Properties and Corollaries

The Completeness Score has several properties that make it a useful metric for data quality assessment.

  1. Range: The Completeness Score is bounded between 0 and 1, where 0 indicates a completely empty dataset and 1 indicates a dataset with no missing values.

  2. Additivity: The Completeness Score of a dataset is the average of the completeness scores of its individual attributes. This property allows for granular analysis of data completeness.

  3. Invariance to Attribute Order: The Completeness Score does not depend on the order of attributes or records in the dataset. This invariance ensures consistency in measurements across different datasets.

Conclusion

The Completeness Score is a simple yet powerful metric for assessing data quality. By quantifying the extent of completeness in a dataset, it provides valuable insights into potential biases and limitations that may arise from missing data. Understanding and applying this metric can significantly enhance the reliability and robustness of data-driven analyses and models.
