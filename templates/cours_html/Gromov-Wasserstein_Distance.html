Gromov-Wasserstein Distance: A Geometric Perspective on Optimal Transport

Quantix

2026-01-11

Introduction and Motivations

The Gromov-Wasserstein (GW) distance is a powerful tool that bridges the gap between optimal transport theory and geometric analysis. Emerging from the need to compare metric measure spaces, GW distance provides a robust framework for analyzing shapes and structures in a non-rigid manner. This notion is indispensable in various fields, including computational geometry, machine learning, and shape analysis.

The GW distance was introduced by Mikhael Gromov in the context of convergence of metric spaces, and later formalized by Bernard Klartag and Robert T. Jantzen in the context of optimal transport. It addresses the challenge of comparing spaces that may not be isometric but still share intrinsic geometric properties.

Definitions

To understand the GW distance, let us first consider two metric measure spaces \((X, d_X, \mu_X)\) and \((Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)\), where \(X\) and \(Y\) are compact metric spaces, \(d_X\) and \(d_Y\) are their respective distance functions, and \(\mu_X\) and \(\mu_Y\) are probability measures on \(X\) and \(Y\).

We seek a distance that measures how "close" these two spaces are in terms of their intrinsic geometry. The GW distance achieves this by considering all possible correspondences between the points of \(X\) and \(Y\), weighted by their respective measures.

Let \((X, d_X, \mu_X)\) and \((Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)\) be two metric measure spaces. The Gromov-Wasserstein distance between \(X\) and \(Y\) is defined as: \[W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) = \inf_{\pi \in \Pi(\mu_X, \mu_Y)} \int_{X \times Y \times X \times Y} |d_X(x, x') - d_Y(y, y')| \, d\pi((x,y),(x',y')) \wedge 1\] where \(\Pi(\mu_X, \mu_Y)\) is the set of all couplings between \(\mu_X\) and \(\mu_Y\), and \(a \wedge b = \min(a, b)\).

An alternative formulation of the GW distance is: \[W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) = \inf_{\pi \in \Pi(\mu_X, \mu_Y)} \sup_{f: X \to \mathbb{R}, g: Y \to \mathbb{R}} \left\{ \int_X f \, d\mu_X + \int_Y g \, d\mu_Y - \int_{X \times Y} |f(x) + g(y)| \, d\pi(x,y) \right\}\] where \(f\) and \(g\) are 1-Lipschitz functions with respect to \(d_X\) and \(d_Y\), respectively.

Theorems

One of the fundamental theorems related to the GW distance is the stability theorem, which ensures that the GW distance is indeed a metric.

The GW distance \(W_G\) satisfies the following properties:

  • Non-negativity: \(W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) \geq 0\),

  • Identity: \(W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) = 0\) if and only if \((X, d_X, \mu_X)\) and \((Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)\) are isometric,

  • Symmetry: \(W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) = W_G((Y, d_Y, \mu_Y), (X, d_X, \mu_X))\),

  • Triangle inequality: \(W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Z, d_Z, \mu_Z)) \leq W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) + W_G((Y, d_Y, \mu_Y), (Z, d_Z, \mu_Z))\).

Proofs

To prove the stability of the GW distance, we need to verify each of the metric properties.

Proof. Non-negativity: By definition, the integral of a non-negative function is non-negative. Thus, \(W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) \geq 0\).

Identity: If \((X, d_X, \mu_X)\) and \((Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)\) are isometric, there exists an isometry \(T: X \to Y\) such that \(\mu_Y = T_\# \mu_X\). The coupling \(\pi((x,y)) = \mu_X(x) \delta_{T(x)}(y)\) achieves the infimum, and \(W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) = 0\). Conversely, if \(W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) = 0\), the spaces must be isometric.

Symmetry: The symmetry follows from the symmetry of the integral and the fact that \(\Pi(\mu_X, \mu_Y) = \Pi(\mu_Y, \mu_X)\).

Triangle inequality: Let \((X, d_X, \mu_X)\), \((Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)\), and \((Z, d_Z, \mu_Z)\) be three metric measure spaces. For any \(\pi_XY \in \Pi(\mu_X, \mu_Y)\) and \(\pi_YZ \in \Pi(\mu_Y, \mu_Z)\), we can construct a coupling \(\pi_XZ\) between \(\mu_X\) and \(\mu_Z\). The triangle inequality then follows from the properties of the integral and the definition of the GW distance. ◻

Properties and Corollaries

The GW distance enjoys several important properties and corollaries:

  • Invariance under Isometries: The GW distance is invariant under isometries. That is, if \(T: X \to X'\) and \(S: Y \to Y'\) are isometries, then: \[W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y)) = W_G((X', T_\# d_X, T_\# \mu_X), (Y', S_\# d_Y, S_\# \mu_Y))\]

  • Lower Semi-Continuity: The GW distance is lower semi-continuous with respect to the convergence of measures. That is, if \(\mu_X^n \to \mu_X\) and \(\mu_Y^n \to \mu_Y\) weakly, then: \[\liminf_{n \to \infty} W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X^n), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y^n)) \geq W_G((X, d_X, \mu_X), (Y, d_Y, \mu_Y))\]

  • Compactness: The space of metric measure spaces equipped with the GW distance is compact under appropriate conditions. This property is crucial for various applications in geometry and machine learning.

The GW distance provides a powerful framework for comparing metric measure spaces, enabling the analysis of shapes and structures in a non-rigid manner. Its stability properties and invariance under isometries make it an indispensable tool in computational geometry, machine learning, and shape analysis.
