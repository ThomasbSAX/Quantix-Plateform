Gini Coefficient: A Measure of Inequality

Quantix

2026-01-11

Introduction and Motivations

The Gini coefficient, introduced by the Italian statistician Corrado Gini in 1912, is a measure of statistical dispersion intended to represent the income or wealth distribution of a nation’s residents. It is often used as a gauge of economic inequality, measuring income distribution among individuals within an economy.

The Gini coefficient ranges from 0 to 1, where 0 represents perfect equality and 1 represents maximal inequality among values. It is often expressed as a percentage between 0 and 100, with 0% expressing perfect equality and 100% expressing maximal inequality.

The Gini coefficient is defined based on the Lorenz curve, which plots the proportion of overall income or wealth assumed by the bottom x% of the population. The Gini coefficient can then be thought of as the ratio of the area between the Lorenz curve and the diagonal (which represents perfect equality) to the total area under the diagonal.

Definitions

To understand the Gini coefficient, we first need to define the Lorenz curve. The Lorenz curve is a graphical representation of income or wealth distribution.

Let \(X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n\) be a set of non-negative values representing incomes or wealths. The Lorenz curve \(L(p)\) is defined as the cumulative proportion of the total income or wealth held by the bottom \(p \times 100\%\) of the population, where \(0 \leq p \leq 1\).

Mathematically, \[L(p) = \frac{1}{\mu} \int_{0}^{p} F^{-1}(u) \, du\] where \(\mu\) is the mean income or wealth, \(F\) is the cumulative distribution function of \(X\), and \(F^{-1}\) is its inverse.

Now, we can define the Gini coefficient based on the Lorenz curve.

The Gini coefficient \(G\) is defined as twice the area between the Lorenz curve and the diagonal line of equality.

Mathematically, \[G = 1 - 2 \int_{0}^{1} L(p) \, dp\] Alternatively, it can be expressed as: \[G = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} |X_i - X_j|}{2n^2 \bar{X}}\] where \(X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n\) are the individual incomes or wealths, \(n\) is the number of individuals, and \(\bar{X}\) is the mean income or wealth.

Theorems

There are several important theorems related to the Gini coefficient. One of the most notable is the following:

The Gini coefficient \(G\) satisfies the following properties:

  1. \(0 \leq G \leq 1\)

  2. If all incomes are equal, \(G = 0\).

  3. If one individual has all the income and others have none, \(G = 1\).

  4. The Gini coefficient is invariant under linear transformations of income.

Proofs

Let’s prove the first property of the Gini coefficient, \(0 \leq G \leq 1\).

Proof. The Lorenz curve \(L(p)\) is a concave function with \(L(0) = 0\) and \(L(1) = 1\). The area between the Lorenz curve and the diagonal line of equality is therefore always non-negative and less than or equal to 0.5.

Mathematically, \[0 \leq \int_{0}^{1} (p - L(p)) \, dp \leq 0.5\]

Multiplying by 2 gives: \[0 \leq 1 - 2 \int_{0}^{1} L(p) \, dp \leq 1\]

Thus, \[0 \leq G \leq 1\] ◻

Properties and Corollaries

The Gini coefficient has several important properties and corollaries:

  1. Invariance under Linear Transformations: The Gini coefficient is invariant under linear transformations of income. That is, if all incomes are multiplied by a constant factor or shifted by a constant amount, the Gini coefficient remains unchanged.

  2. Decomposability: The Gini coefficient can be decomposed into within-group and between-group inequality components. This allows for the analysis of inequality at different levels, such as within countries or regions and between them.

  3. Sensitivity to Transfers: The Gini coefficient is sensitive to transfers of income or wealth between individuals. Specifically, it captures the impact of such transfers on overall inequality.

Let’s prove the invariance under linear transformations.

Proof. Consider a linear transformation of income \(Y_i = aX_i + b\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants. The mean income after the transformation is \(\bar{Y} = a\bar{X} + b\).

The Gini coefficient for \(Y\) is: \[G_Y = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} |Y_i - Y_j|}{2n^2 \bar{Y}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} |aX_i + b - aX_j - b|}{2n^2 (a\bar{X} + b)} = \frac{a \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} |X_i - X_j|}{2n^2 a (\bar{X} + \frac{b}{a})} = G_X\]

Thus, the Gini coefficient remains unchanged under linear transformations of income. ◻

Conclusion

The Gini coefficient is a powerful tool for measuring economic inequality. Its properties and theoretical foundations make it a valuable instrument in the study of income and wealth distribution. Understanding its definition, theorems, proofs, and properties provides a comprehensive view of this important statistical measure.
