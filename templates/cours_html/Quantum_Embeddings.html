Introduction and Motivations

Quantum embeddings represent a fascinating intersection of quantum computing and classical machine learning. The emergence of this concept is driven by the need to efficiently represent high-dimensional quantum states in classical systems, enabling the application of classical optimization techniques to quantum problems. This bridging mechanism is indispensable for hybrid quantum-classical algorithms, which leverage the strengths of both paradigms to tackle complex computational tasks.

The concept of quantum embeddings is rooted in the principles of quantum information theory and classical data representation. By embedding quantum states into a classical framework, we can exploit well-established classical optimization methods to solve problems that are inherently quantum in nature. This approach is particularly relevant in the context of noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) devices, where classical optimization plays a crucial role in mitigating errors and improving algorithm performance.

Definitions

To understand quantum embeddings, we first need to grasp the notion of a quantum state and its classical representation. Consider a quantum system described by a state vector \(|\psi\rangle\) in a Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\). Our goal is to find a classical representation of this quantum state that preserves essential properties such as distances and correlations.

A quantum state \(|\psi\rangle\) is a normalized vector in a complex Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\), i.e., \[|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H} \quad \text{such that} \quad \langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 1.\]

A quantum embedding is a mapping \(\mathcal{E}: \mathcal{H} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n\) that transforms a quantum state \(|\psi\rangle\) into a classical vector \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n\). Formally, \[\mathcal{E}(|\psi\rangle) = \mathbf{x} \quad \text{where} \quad \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n.\] The embedding should preserve relevant properties of the quantum state, such as distances and correlations.

Theorems

One of the fundamental theorems in quantum embeddings is the Quantum-to-Classical Embedding Theorem, which ensures that certain properties of quantum states are preserved under the embedding.

For any quantum state \(|\psi\rangle\) in a Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\), there exists an embedding \(\mathcal{E}\) such that the inner product between two states is preserved: \[|\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle| = \frac{|\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2|}{(\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_1)(\mathbf{x}_2 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2)}.\] Here, \(\mathbf{x}_1 = \mathcal{E}(|\psi_1\rangle)\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2 = \mathcal{E}(|\psi_2\rangle)\).

Proofs

The proof of the Quantum-to-Classical Embedding Theorem relies on the properties of inner products and the preservation of distances under the embedding.

Proof. Consider two quantum states \(|\psi_1\rangle\) and \(|\psi_2\rangle\) in a Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}\). The inner product between these states is given by: \[\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle = \sum_{i} c_i^* d_i,\] where \(|\psi_1\rangle = \sum_{i} c_i |i\rangle\) and \(|\psi_2\rangle = \sum_{i} d_i |i\rangle\).

Under the embedding \(\mathcal{E}\), these states are mapped to classical vectors \(\mathbf{x}_1\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2\). The dot product between these vectors is: \[\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2 = \sum_{i} x_{1,i} x_{2,i}.\]

To preserve the inner product, we require: \[|\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle| = \frac{|\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2|}{(\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_1)(\mathbf{x}_2 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2)}.\]

This condition ensures that the relative distances and correlations between quantum states are preserved in their classical representations. ◻

Properties and Corollaries

Quantum embeddings possess several important properties that make them useful in hybrid quantum-classical algorithms.

For any two quantum states \(|\psi_1\rangle\) and \(|\psi_2\rangle\), the distance between their classical embeddings \(\mathbf{x}_1\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2\) is proportional to the quantum distance: \[\|\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_2\| = k \cdot \|\psi_1 - \psi_2\|,\] where \(k\) is a proportionality constant.

Proof. The distance between two quantum states is given by: \[\|\psi_1 - \psi_2\| = \sqrt{\langle\psi_1 - \psi_2|\psi_1 - \psi_2\rangle}.\]

Under the embedding, this distance is preserved up to a proportionality constant \(k\): \[\|\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_2\| = k \cdot \sqrt{\langle\psi_1 - \psi_2|\psi_1 - \psi_2\rangle}.\]

This ensures that the geometric structure of the quantum state space is maintained in its classical representation. ◻

For any two quantum states \(|\psi_1\rangle\) and \(|\psi_2\rangle\), the correlation between their classical embeddings \(\mathbf{x}_1\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2\) is proportional to the quantum correlation: \[\frac{\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2}{\|\mathbf{x}_1\| \|\mathbf{x}_2\|} = k \cdot \frac{|\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle|}{\sqrt{\langle\psi_1|\psi_1\rangle \langle\psi_2|\psi_2\rangle}}.\]

Proof. The correlation between two quantum states is given by: \[\frac{|\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle|}{\sqrt{\langle\psi_1|\psi_1\rangle \langle\psi_2|\psi_2\rangle}}.\]

Under the embedding, this correlation is preserved up to a proportionality constant \(k\): \[\frac{\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2}{\|\mathbf{x}_1\| \|\mathbf{x}_2\|} = k \cdot \frac{|\langle\psi_1|\psi_2\rangle|}{\sqrt{\langle\psi_1|\psi_1\rangle \langle\psi_2|\psi_2\rangle}}.\]

This ensures that the relationships between quantum states are maintained in their classical representations. ◻

Conclusion

Quantum embeddings provide a powerful framework for bridging the gap between quantum and classical computing. By preserving essential properties of quantum states in their classical representations, we can leverage classical optimization techniques to solve complex quantum problems. This approach is particularly relevant in the context of NISQ devices, where classical optimization plays a crucial role in mitigating errors and improving algorithm performance. As quantum computing continues to evolve, the development of efficient and accurate quantum embeddings will be essential for unlocking the full potential of hybrid quantum-classical algorithms.
